At What Point Is Behavior Considered Sexual Harassment?

Although federal law doesn't prohibit simple teasing, offhand comments, or isolated incidents that are not very serious, harassment is illegal when it is so frequent or severe that it creates a hostile or offensive work environment and interferes with another individual's work.

Some states have even broader standards for when a behavior is considered harassment.

California

  • California protects employees against harassment based on any protected characteristic and follows the federal standard in evaluating such claims. Under California law, an employee claiming hostile work environment harassment based on a protected characteristic must demonstrate that the conduct complained of was severe enough or sufficiently pervasive to alter the conditions of employment and create a work environment that qualifies as hostile or abusive.

Connecticut

  • Connecticut protects employees against harassment based on sex or gender identity or expression and follows the federal standard in evaluating such claims. Under Connecticut law, an employee claiming harassment based on a hostile work environment must demonstrate that the conduct complained of was severe enough or sufficiently pervasive to alter the conditions of employment and create a work environment that qualifies as hostile or abusive.

New York

  • Protections against sexual harassment are even broader under the New York City Human Rights Law ("NYCHRL"). In order to demonstrate sexual harassment under the NYCHRL, an employee only needs to prove that he or she was treated less well because of gender and that the treatment was more than a petty slight or trivial inconvenience.
  • Under New York State Law, harassment is an unlawful discriminatory practice that subjects an individual to inferior terms, conditions or privileges of employment because of the employee's membership in a protected category. To be unlawful, harassing conduct must rise above the level of what a reasonable victim of discrimination with the same protected characteristic(s) would consider petty slights or trivial inconveniences.
